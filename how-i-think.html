The Operational Manual to My Mind

How Daniel Wallen Thinks About Life

Exercise is great, but your brain is the most important muscle in your body.

No pressure to become a philosopher. That said, ponder the important stuff.

Self-reflection will build mental connections that lead to better life decisions.

Soon, you’ll have a mental framework that guides your decision-making process.

To see what I mean, read the operational manual to my mind (#3 is odd but effective).

Note: this essay was the result of deep meditation and retrospection (a LOT of it).

Then I added depth + actionable steps. And changed to a second person POV.

Want to see the mental model I use as a moral compass? Scroll below…

1. Change what you can. Accept what you can’t.

Circumstances restrict your ability to move.

In time, any obstacle will fall. But it takes a while.

Accept the job you need now. Build the business you want later.

“Later” doesn’t mean procrastinate forever. It means be patient.

Hustle is important, but it’s not about an endless stream of movement.

Hunters and fishers spend most of their day waiting. Life works the same way.

Detectives and private I’s follow 100 trails to nowhere before they close the case.

Accept the fact any outcome demands an unknown amount of time and frustration.

Meanwhile, leverage your skills and talents in a way that attracts paid opportunities.

2. Pause, breathe, and think before you speak.

A code or mission should inform every decision you make.

Decisions include how you respond to any form of correspondence.

Apply this reasoning to phone calls and in-person conversations, too.

Whether it’s a friend or business partner, listen to their words and tone.

Reflect for a second and structure your response to fit the precise context.

If there’s potential confusion, rephrase and ask follow-up questions to confirm.

They’ll appreciate your effort to understand and share details you should know.

Don’t forget to breathe. Deep breathing helps you focus and live in the moment.

3. Schedule a daily panic session (seriously).

In a perfect world, you’d never panic. We don’t live in a perfect world.

Bad things happen to good people. Risk can be minimized (not eliminated).

You’ll run into difficult people and stressful situations that rattle your nerves.

Deep inhales + slow exhales = the way. For brownie points, add a brief pause.

Swallow the stress and promise yourself: “It’s okay, I will deal with this later.”

Open a small timeslot – 5-15 minutes – where you’re free to panic and worry.

“Pause” negative emotions until you can deal with them in a positive fashion.

4. Be your own priest and/or confessional.

Nobody’s perfect. You’ll say or do the wrong thing. Feelings will get hurt. Oops.

In business, this stuff is easy to avoid with sanity and systems or processes.

Conflicts are harder to avoid with friends, family, and romantic partners.

There’s no red tape or corporate rules to guide the language you use.

And you need to talk through certain issues, uncomfortable or not.

Heated discussions are appropriate, but do your best to be cool.

Don’t select a phrase designed to be hurtful (borderline evil).

Stay calm and civil. Focus on the facts and relevant details.

Theory is easy. Application is hard. Read: you’ll mess up.

Apologize, but be gentle with yourself. You’re human.

Forgive yourself of any perceived sins every Sunday.

5. Focus on foundational principles first.

Tactics are useless without a solid strategy.

Strategy must be informed by an awareness of foundations.

Fat loss is a good example. It doesn’t matter how complex your workout is.

If you aren’t in a caloric deficit, the best routine ever invented can’t save you.

The same theory applies to websites. Content is more important than web design.

A beautiful website with bad copy will lose to an ugly website with good copy.

Identify the biggest levers to pull and prioritize them above all else.

6. Document everything you do in depth.

Systems and processes give you superpowers. They provide an ability to clone yourself.

Run a business that requires you to collect email leads? This is a good starting point.

Create a training manual with detailed instructions and outsource that activity.

No biz? That’s okay. It’s not for everybody. You can still have a clone army.

Housecleaning and grocery shopping could be outsourced with ease.

Use the time savings to pursue a hobby or bond with your family.

Look for things to automate, such as auto drafts for your bills.

7. Edit and evolve until the day you die.

No first draft becomes a bestselling book.

Bestselling books are a result of endless revisions.

Editing is a form of evolution that creates masterpieces.

Apply the same reasoning to your life. It can be edited at anytime.

Learn, grow, and revise your personal story until it becomes a masterpiece.

8. There are many paths to any outcome.

The goal or outcome is all that matters. Don’t be attached to the means of achieving it.

For example, I took a gap year with the goal of becoming a developer or engineer.

People have been led to believe this is the only path to a new career in tech.

This is unfortunate, because the hiring process for devs is a total nightmare.

You have to practice code challenges for hundreds or thousands of hours.

Otherwise, there’s zero hope of getting through the technical interviews.

These challenges teach you nothing about building apps or websites.

I respect my time; and thus, am unwilling to play this stupid game.

I found a better game. One I can win. Be willing to do the same.

9. Pivot when you find a faster/easier way.

Laziness is often perceived as a bad thing.

If you spend all your time watching TV, it’s true.

On the other hand, laziness can boost productivity.

Why not seek a faster and easier way to get things done?

This is why I pivoted to sales and support engineer positions.

As an actor and writer (strong communicator), they play to my strengths.

And you’ll never be asked to traverse a binary search tree in reverse (silly).

After I adopted this new strategy, I went from no interviews to 3-5 every week.

Don’t feel guilty for abandoning a plan and replacing it with a more efficient one.

10. Preserve all the time and energy you can.

Time and energy are the most important resources any human has at their disposal.

Money is a result of pointing your time and energy in the most strategic direction.

Happiness is a result of spending your time and energy on the right activities.

If you’re broke or unhappy, your time and energy aren’t being utilized well.

11. Optimize for the most important objective.

Go “All In” for one primary objective at a time.

Getting a job. Finding a client. Launching a business.

All of these goals require an extremely different approach.

Pursue more than one objective and people will be confused.

Align your social media profiles and content in the same direction.

After you check off the most important task, realign them in a new direction.

The world is yours. You’re capable of anything. But focus on one thing for now.

12. Package your best insights into a single unit.

Incredible content is a secret weapon. It helps you stand out from the crowd.

Don’t be shy. It’s okay to flex! Why not impress people you want to work with?

Show people you have the most essential soft skill: an ability to communicate.

Use the same skill to produce books and online courses about your expertise.

You won’t have to seek opportunities. They’ll eventually come to you.

13. Present yourself in the #1 most positive light.

Creeping is the new normal.

Be your own public relations firm.

Pretend you have a PR agent for a second.

Would they be horrified by your social media updates?

If so, you might be sending the wrong message to the world.

Save controversial takes for private conversations and friends-only posts.

Another option is to put that stuff in long-form content with lots of context.

Don’t give a potential client or employer any reason to say: “That’s a big nope.”

14. Connect with your greatest heroes and heroines.

Your favorite actor, author, or artist? They’re a normal person just like you.

I sent a letter to my favorite author: Dean Koontz. Guess what? He replied.

The man has outsold Stephen King and yet he took the time to write back.

Once, I quoted Sarah Michelle Gellar in a blog, and tagged her in a share.

Guess what? She liked and retweeted the article to her many followers.

The guy who played Michael Myers in Halloween met me for breakfast.

I asked him to sign a comic book in a letter. “I’ll do you one better,” he said.

15. Build the project no other human could possibly do.

Copycats are lame. Stealing ideas because they’re incapable of having an original one.

No one wants to read a book or article that’s been published a thousand times.

Everyone wants to read fresh new insights they can’t find anywhere else.

You don’t have to disrupt the foundations of your industry or anything.

But you must find a unique spin that fits your individual perspective.

16. Develop your skills, talents, thoughts, and ideas everyday.

Skills and talents must be developed.

Thoughts and ideas must be sharpened.

Otherwise, they’ll become dull and irrelevant.

Progress doesn’t come to a halt after you graduate.

Growth doesn’t cease to exist after you become an adult.

Whether you’re 16 or 60, there’s no excuse to stop learning.

Fail to listen and folks who evolve daily will run laps around you.

17. Select a career that provides fulfillment and growth potential.

Work consumes more time than any other aspect of life (unless you have a trust fund).

Given this reality, why would you settle for a job that makes you feel miserable?

It’s never too late. I’ve had classmates who were old enough to be my mother.

Employers who can’t provide upward mobility should be abandoned ASAP.

They’re a parasite who feeds on your life without providing any benefit.

Raise the bar for yourself and the work environments you’ll accept.

Live in a small town? I know that feel. Analyze remote possibilities.

You’re no longer limited by geographical boundaries. Be creative.

18. Choose a cause where you can make a BIG dent in the Universe.

The best cause?

One where you can make a real impact.

Arguing about politics doesn’t fit the bill.

Often, you’ll merely harden their existing opinion.

Only persuasive hypnotists change hearts and minds.

You have to work through decades of mental programming.

Better to focus on a cause where you can create tangible results.

This is why I walk shelter dogs and “advertise” them with Facebook posts.

Instant result: the dog is happy to take a field trip and get away from their kennel.

Delayed result: the dog often finds their forever family (150+ adoptions and counting!).

19. Find a career-to-cause ratio that provides a secure, healthy balance.

Devoting your time and energy to a cause is good. Overdoing it can end badly.

Money isn’t everything. But there’s no denying it provides true freedom.

The sooner you’re secure, the sooner you can do more for the world.

In many cases, writing a check for $1,000 > volunteering 10 hours.

Earn more today so you can be more generous in the future.

20. Explore and experiment with every recipe until your life tastes delicious.

Take nothing in this essay as Gospel Truth.

Telling you how to think about life isn’t the goal.

The goal = encourage you to reflect about your own principles.

The firmer your principles, the more confident you’ll be about your choices.

Write an operational manual for your own mind to make better decisions everyday.
